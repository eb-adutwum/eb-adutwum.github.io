The Process of Knowing: A Guide to Learning

Reflections on how we learn, why we learn, and what it means to truly know.

What is Learning?

Learning simply means the process of knowing something. Two things worthy of noting from this definition are process of knowing and the something. Learning leads to knowledge, through which we trigger intelligence by applying what we know or have learned and changing what we thought was great into something even better. All of this is built on the infrastructure of wisdom, which to me is far more immaterial and spiritual - a gift from God [James 1:17].

Consider a 1-year-old child who is developing the ability to walk. First, they mimic what they see others do (observation). Then they try it themselves (intelligence). They stumble and fall (experience), so they start holding on to objects (adjustment), and eventually take their first steps. Repeat this long enough and the child is walking. The difference in how fast two children learn to walk may be due to intelligence (IQ, adaptability), but there is also an inner impulse - a kind of wisdom - that drives them forward.

Why Do We Learn?

The child's example highlights a critical reason for learning: survival and growth. Learning helps us pass down information essential to life - walking, eating, speaking. As we grow, we use learning to gain literacy, professional skills, or even fashion and cooking. At higher levels, we learn to innovate and create: designing cars, writing software, inventing medical technology. Life continues and improves because we learn. In short: everything we do in life, we learn somehow—good or bad.

How Do We Learn?

Humans have many ways to perceive information - sight, hearing, smell, touch, taste, and even emotions. Any of these can work alone or together, creating over 50 possible combinations for perception. But perception alone is not learning.

True learning happens when we interact with information deeply enough that we can apply it beyond its original context. That is why schools test knowledge using varied problems and why brilliant thinkers explain complex ideas through analogies.

Learning requires synthesis and interaction. This is where many struggle - especially in academic settings, where observation is limited, time is short, and failure carries a cost. Unlike the child learning to walk (whose failures do not "count"), students often feel penalized for failure, making learning harder.

Learning in Practice

In academics, learning is framed around studying - giving full attention, time, and effort to acquiring knowledge. It includes sourcing, perceiving, practicing, and retaining information.

The challenge today is time and distraction. We often hesitate to invest effort, fearing it may yield nothing. But learning remains the only path to true knowing, whether in school, work, or life.
